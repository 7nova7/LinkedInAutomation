### Key Insights

1. **Agents vs. Chatbots - Fundamentally Different**: Agentic systems go beyond reactive question-answering; they autonomously break down problems, plan solutions, execute actions, and iterate. This represents a leap from simple LLM chatbots to self-directed systems.

   - Evidence: The assignment mandates a "rigorous mini-benchmark" with 2+ test cases, defined success criteria, failure analysis, and scoring rubrics as "one of the most important parts for a successful agent."
   - LinkedIn angle: Professionals building AI solutions must move beyond hype and adopt engineering rigor; quality assurance makes or breaks agent deployments.

3. **Skills and Modular Design Enable Scalability**: The course promotes a "Skills Pack" architecture where discrete, reusable skill modules can be combined to solve different problems—allowing teams to build once and apply widely.
   - Evidence: Students can choose to document either a Skills Pack (modular agents) or an n8n/SDK workflow, both emphasizing composable, maintainable architecture.
   - LinkedIn angle: Enterprises benefit from modular AI systems that reduce duplication and scale faster than monolithic solutions.

4. **Real-World Iteration Drives Success**: Successful agents emerge through testing against real inputs, documenting failures, and refining prompts iteratively—not through theoretical perfection upfront.
   - Evidence: The grading rubric emphasizes "Real Usage + Iteration" (2 pts) and requires clear evidence of 2+ real runs with documented changes and reflections.
   - LinkedIn angle: Teams adopting agentic AI should embrace an experimental mindset; transparency about bottlenecks and iterations builds credibility.

   - Evidence: The assignment examples include homework generators, assignment rubrics, and tutorial systems that demonstrate agentic principles in pedagogical contexts.
   - LinkedIn angle: Educators and EdTech leaders should recognize agentic AI as a lever for personalized, scalable learning experiences.

6. **Transparency in Design Builds Trust**: Detailed documentation of problem statements, architectural decisions, prompts, and benchmarking methodology makes systems explainable and reproducible.
   - Evidence: Tutorial guidelines require explicit problem framing, system design documentation, prompt iterations, and honest discussion of bottlenecks.
   - LinkedIn angle: As AI systems gain influence, transparency becomes a competitive advantage and stakeholder confidence booster.

I spent weeks building what I thought was an impressive AI agent.

It had all the pieces: smart prompts, integrated tools, a clean workflow. I was confident. Then I tried to actually test it against real scenarios.

Everything fell apart.

The agent hallucinated on edge cases. It took weird detours when prompts were slightly ambiguous. It failed silently in ways I hadn't anticipated.

That's when I realized something: the hard part of agentic AI isn't the language model. It's the system design.

Most conversations about AI focus on capability—how smart is the model, what can it do. But I've learned that's only half the battle. The other half is rigor. Real, boring, engineer-level rigor: designing benchmarks, stress-testing against failure modes, documenting what breaks and why.

The teams getting real wins with AI agents aren't the ones obsessing over the latest model. They're the ones who:

→ Test against real-world scenarios (not hypotheticals)
→ Build reusable components instead of one-off solutions
→ Measure success with clear metrics, not vibes
→ Iterate openly when things don't work

I'm watching a shift happen. From "Can we build this with AI?" to "Can we build this well with AI?" That second question requires discipline.

If you're exploring agents for your business or team, I'd push you to start with evaluation, not capability. Define what success looks like before you build. Your future self will thank you.

What's been the biggest surprise you've hit when moving from prototype to production with AI?
